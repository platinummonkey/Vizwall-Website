UTSA opens high-tech visual lab

Tech empowers researchers to see, touch in new ways.

By Melissa Ludwig mludwig@express-news.net

Updated 12:02 a.m., Wednesday, May 18, 2011

At the University of Texas at San Antonio's new Advanced Visualization Laboratory, visitors can administer a shot to a patient - without the patient or the shot.

Clutching a pen-like device, visitors move their hands to guide a needle on a computer screen, and the "haptic" device reproduces the sensation as the needle pierces skin.

"Oh man, that is what it feels like. I used to give my wife shots," said Steve Sofhauser, who attended the lab's grand opening Tuesday.

In addition to haptic devices, the lab features a 3-D television and a visualization wall of Dell monitors that stands 141/2 feet wide and 6 feet tall.

Together, they allow researchers to see and touch the digital models they create in the course of research.

Funded with a three-year, $482,600 grant from the National Science Foundation, the lab could be useful for just about any field, from molecular biology to art and music.

With 98 megapixels, the visualization wall can display large-scale, high-definition pictures such as a cityscape, and zoom to the level where you see people walking on the street.

It can display entire galaxies, or magnify a Van Gogh painting to reveal every crack and brush stroke. It can also simulate a beating heart and blood flow through vessels to help doctors make precise treatment decisions.

"Facilities like this stretch us to the future," said John Frederick, UTSA's provost. "It is a terrific opportunity for students to see the world in new ways."

For researchers, it can give form and shape to data that would otherwise be numbers on a spreadsheet. For instance, one research group built a visual model of the way nanoparticles of titanium dioxide cluster in water, which can help determine the environmental effect of products such as sunscreen and makeup.

The lab could have industry uses as well, said Ricardo Romo, president of UTSA, such as training crane operators before putting them in an expensive and dangerous machine.

"I feel like it's the dawning of a new era," Romo said. "We are doing some advanced, innovative work."

To learn more about the Advanced Visualization Laboratory or to schedule a tour, visit http://viswall.utsa.edu.
